European Electric Vehicle Adoption and Trends

Introduction

In recent years, the automotive industry has witnessed a significant shift towards sustainable transportation solutions, driven by concerns over environmental sustainability and the need to reduce carbon emissions (European Environment Agency, 2016). According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), road transport electrification on a global scale is expected to unlock substantial emission reductions in the coming decades (IEA, 2024). By 2035, the switch to electric vehicles (EVs) is projected to avoid over 2 gigatons (Gt) of carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions on a well-to-wheels basis. While there are concerns about additional emissions from electricity generation for EVs, the net savings from transitioning to electric vehicles far outweigh any potential drawbacks.

Government Spending on EV

The visualization in these two graphs shows how government spending has increased overall in past years. One could easily rationalize that with more money, people are more willing to purchase EVs, causing the general rising trend in EV sales worldwide. However, the average spending per EV refutes this by demonstrating a downward trend. Together with the previous visualization, users can notice that with less incentive given for one EV by the government, overall sales of EVs have still been increasing in the past few years, suggesting that monetary factors are not, or at least not the only factors influencing EV sales.

Reasons for People's EV Purchase

This visualization reveals the fundamental reasons behind people’s willingness to purchase EVs. On the left side, the graph describes the reasons inhibiting the purchase of EVs. It is evident that concerns about the lack of charging stations in the city take first place, whereas the lack of charging infrastructure at work or home is less of a concern. On the contrary, the graph on the right illustrates the reasons motivating the purchase of EVs. Interestingly, monetary incentives, which one might assume to be the most influential factor, only take third place. Environmental concerns are the most motivating factor encouraging people’s purchases. Overall, the factors can be categorized into four types: environmental concern, monetary incentives, charging infrastructure, and upfront costs.

Charging Infrastructure and EV Market Share

Previous studies have shown that charging infrastructure is a key factor in the adoption and satisfaction of electric vehicle users. By investigating the relationship between EV market share and selected variables in 30 countries, Sierzchula et al. found a significant positive correlation between charging infrastructure and a country's EV market share. For this part of the study, we have two charts: Figure 2 represents the number of high-speed charging posts per 1000 people, and Figure 1 represents the number of total public charging posts per 1000 people (both high-speed and low-speed charging posts included). As two countries with high EV ratios, both in number and quality of charging posts, Norway and the Netherlands are much higher than Belgium and Spain.

Interestingly, Figure 1 shows a higher density of charging posts in the Netherlands than in Norway. However, the EV share is higher in Norway than in the Netherlands. We believe the difference in the number of high-speed charging posts might explain this. High-speed chargers tend to be more attractive to customers who travel long distances, often installed in highway service areas. Thus, increasing the number of high-speed charging posts may effectively increase the share of EV sales.
